Common Mistakes in OET Speaking and How to Avoid Them

The OET Speaking sub-test offers a unique approach by assessing healthcare professionals in realistic clinical scenarios. While this test is more aligned with your professional experience, there are still common mistakes that candidates make, which can impact their performance. Understanding these mistakes and learning how to avoid them will help you maximize your score and succeed in the OET Speaking sub-test. Here are some frequent mistakes and strategies to avoid them.

1. Overusing Medical Jargon

Mistake: One common error is using too much medical jargon when explaining conditions, treatments, or procedures. While you may be familiar with medical terminology, your patients may not understand it, leading to confusion.

How to Avoid It: Use simple, layman's terms when explaining medical concepts to the patient. If a term must be used, follow it up with a brief explanation. For example, instead of saying "You have hypertension," say, "You have high blood pressure, which means the force of your blood against your blood vessels is higher than normal."

2. Speaking Too Quickly or Too Slowly

Mistake: Some candidates tend to rush through their responses out of nervousness, while others speak too slowly in an attempt to be clear. Both extremes can negatively impact your fluency and the patient's understanding.

How to Avoid It: Practice speaking at a natural, conversational pace. Make sure you are not rushing through explanations or pausing excessively. If you notice yourself speeding up, take a deep breath and slow down. On the other hand, if you are speaking too slowly, try practicing with a timer to improve your pacing.

3. Failing to Build Rapport

Mistake: Jumping straight into clinical information without establishing a connection can make the conversation feel impersonal. In a healthcare setting, this approach might leave patients feeling uneasy or unheard.

How to Avoid It: Begin the conversation with a warm greeting, use the patient’s name, and show genuine interest in their feelings or concerns. For example, start with, "Hello, Mr. Smith. How are you feeling today?" This helps build rapport and sets a positive tone for the rest of the conversation.

4. Not Checking for Understanding

Mistake: Some candidates give information without checking if the patient understands. This can lead to the patient feeling overwhelmed or confused, especially when receiving complex medical information.

How to Avoid It: Regularly pause during explanations to check if the patient is following. You can ask questions like, "Does that make sense?" or "Do you have any questions about what I just explained?" This encourages the patient to engage and ensures they are clear on the information.

5. Missing the Patient’s Concerns

Mistake: Overlooking the patient’s emotions, concerns, or expectations is another common error. Focusing solely on clinical tasks may result in missing important cues about what the patient is worried about.

How to Avoid It: Pay attention to any verbal or non-verbal signs indicating the patient’s concerns. Ask open-ended questions like, "How do you feel about this procedure?" or "Is there anything you're worried about?" This helps you understand the patient's perspective and address their specific concerns.

6. Disorganized Explanations

Mistake: Providing information in a jumbled or disorganized manner can make it difficult for the patient to follow your explanation. This can lead to confusion and decreased understanding.

How to Avoid It: Use a structured approach when explaining medical information. Signpost the key points (e.g., "First, I’ll explain what the test is for. Then, I’ll talk about what happens during the test.") and make sure to provide information in a logical sequence. This makes it easier for the patient to understand and retain what you are saying.

7. Over-Reliance on the Role-Play Card

Mistake: While the role-play card provides necessary information, some candidates read directly from it or rely too heavily on it, resulting in a lack of natural interaction.

How to Avoid It: Use the role-play card as a guide, not a script. Familiarize yourself with the details during the preparation time and then focus on interacting naturally with the interlocutor. Practice speaking in a conversational manner, as you would with a real patient, rather than reading directly from the card.

8. Interrupting or Talking Over the Patient

Mistake: Interrupting the patient or not allowing them to finish their thoughts can make the patient feel disregarded. This can negatively impact the rapport and the flow of the conversation.

How to Avoid It: Always let the patient finish speaking before responding. This shows that you value what they have to say and helps create a positive interaction. If you need to clarify something, wait until they have completed their sentence.

Conclusion

Avoiding these common mistakes will significantly enhance your performance in the OET Speaking sub-test. Focus on clear communication, building rapport, and structuring your explanations effectively. These skills not only help you pass the OET but also prepare you for real-world healthcare scenarios where effective communication is vital for patient care. Practice regularly, seek feedback, and incorporate these strategies to ensure a confident and successful OET Speaking test experience.
